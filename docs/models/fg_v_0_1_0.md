## Stage 1: Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA)

Before writing any modeling code, we performed a comprehensive empirical audit of NFL play-by-play data from the **2016 to 2025 seasons** (representing 10,731 total valid field goal attempts) fetched via `nfl_data_py`. This analysis establishes the baseline priors, success curves, and temporal trends governing field goal success.

### 1. Summary Statistics (Baseline Priors)

Across all seasons in the EDA window (2016–2025), we observed the following baseline stats:

*   **Total FG Attempts:** 10,731
*   **Total Made:** 9,085
*   **Overall Success Rate:** 84.66% (prior rate with no distance conditioning)
*   **Kick Distance Range:** 18 to 69 yards
*   **Mean Kick Distance:** 37.8 yards
*   **Median Kick Distance:** 38.0 yards
*   **Standard Deviation:** 10.4 yards

### 2. Distance Distribution

Field goal attempt frequencies are not uniform. The bulk of attempts are clustered between 20 and 48 yards. Kicks shorter than 20 yards (extremely close goal-line situations) and kicks longer than 55 yards (extreme range) are rare.

### 3. Empirical Success Curve (5-Yard Bins)

Accuracies stay above 95% for kicks under 30 yards, then decay in a non-linear fashion as distance increases, dropping below 75% at 43 yards, and falling to ~28% for attempts from 63–67 yards. Kicks from 68+ yards have a 0% success rate in our historical sample.

## Stage 2: Modeling Decision

### 1. The Chosen Approach: Single-Variable Logistic Regression

We formally evaluated two candidate machine learning models for this task:
1.  **Logistic Regression** on `kick_distance` (fitted in log-odds space using a standard sigmoid link).
2.  **XGBoost Classifier** on `kick_distance` (constrained to `n_estimators=50` and `max_depth=3`).

We chose **Logistic Regression** as the winner.

#### Why Logistic Regression over XGBoost?

1.  **Strict Monotonicity Constraint**: A fundamental physical rule of football is that longer kicks are strictly harder than shorter kicks. XGBoost is non-parametric and step-wise; during evaluation, XGBoost predicted that a 35-yard kick is easier than a 33-yard kick due to local sample variance in the training data. Logistic Regression mathematically guarantees strict, smooth monotonicity across all distances.
2.  **Realistic Extrapolation**: For downstream 4th-down decision logic (EV calculations), the model must predict realistic probabilities at extreme distances (e.g., 65–75 yards). Because XGBoost splits on discrete thresholds, it predicted an 18.55% success rate at 70 yards because the last leaf node extended to infinity. Logistic Regression naturally decays to 0%, predicting a realistic 20.17% at 70 yards and 2.2% at 85 yards.
3.  **Parsimonious Calibration**: Logistic Regression optimizes log loss (which directly calibrates probabilities). Since our downstream engine relies on EV, probability calibration is the primary optimization objective.

### 6. Playcalling Heuristics & Constraints (Model Registry)

The downstream playcalling engine (`ModelRegistry.predict_4th_down_probas`) coordinates coach decisions with these rules:
1.  **Normal Play Limit**: Kicking a field goal longer than 65 yards (`yardline_100 > 48`) is strictly prohibited. The playcalling engine redirects choices to Punt or Go for it.
2.  **Desperation Override**: At the end of Q2 (halftime <= 30 seconds left) or Q4 (game ending <= 30 seconds left, trailing by <= 3 points or tied), coaches are permitted to attempt a field goal up to 70 yards (`yardline_100 <= 53`) as a desperation attempt. These attempts have a 0% success probability for now (pending 2027 Kicker DNA models), but correctly represent desperation situations.
3.  **Mandatory Touchdown**: Under 2 minutes remaining, if a team is trailing by 4 to 8 points, they are forced to go for it on 4th down regardless of field position.

### 7. Vectorized Array Inference Support (V.0.2.0 Upgrade)

To support the vectorized simulation engine (`VectorizedNFLGameEngine`) and prevent correlated parallel game results (the "identical scoring" issue where games locked step on field goal success), `FieldGoalModelV010.predict_success_probability` was upgraded to accept both scalar inputs and numpy arrays vectorially:
* **Vectorized Signature:** `predict_success_probability(self, yardline_100, kicker_multiplier=1.0)`
* **Vectorized Optimization:** When passed a numpy array of yardlines, it computes the kick distances, applies raw sigmoid matrix weights, overlays kicker multipliers, clips to logical bounds `[0.0, 1.0]`, and filters out kicks > 65 yards vectorially using numpy masking (`prob[kick_distance > 65.0] = 0.0`). This guarantees high-speed computation (sub-microsecond) and independent game random rolling.
